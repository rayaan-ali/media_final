What It Means to Be a Bad Cree

A “Bad Cree” is someone disconnected from their culture, dreams, and family. Mackenzie’s journey is the reclamation of all 3 and Bad Cree proves Indigenous stories can reclaim what silence tried to erase.
Rayaan Ali | Bad Cree by Jessica Johns

A murder of crows travelling together as a family (Brumble)

When land is taken, so is everything that grew from it, from the language, and stories to ceremonies and ourselves.

Reclamation in the Present

To reconnect back with lost identity, reclamation takes place and to reclaim something that is rightfully yours, you must use your voice to express the feelings you have right over. This is Mackenzie's story, a young Cree woman who left her community for Vancouver and found herself between two worlds, haunted by dreams she couldn't understand as she lost the cultural knowledge needed.

Mackenzie’s journey to reclaiming her identity is a reclamation much similar to the current experiences of Indigenous people. Currently, urbanization disconnects people from the land leading to a loss of language and culture. Over 47% of Indigenous individuals residing in urban centers feel loneliness, a direct link to their disconnection to identity and culture. As to reclaiming their identity, Indigenous people actively utilize Canadian government funding to programs such as the National Association of Friendship Centers in 2024 to support Indigenous language and culture in urban spaces. In Bad Cree Mackenzie’s own reclamation when she chooses to stop running from her dreams, and faces it with her family.

Reclamation Through Family and Sisters

"You aren't ravens," Mom would say. "You are crows. You travel together everywhere."

(Johns 169)

The crows are both a part of the physical and dream world. At the start of the novel, Mackenzie is a raven being isolated in Vancouver, disconnected from her sisters, avoiding her dreams and grief. Her mom’s words remind us of what Mackenzie has lost and what she must reclaim. The crows themselves serve as a physical reminder to how the three sisters are connected like a murder travelling through a journey.

"If there's one thing you're taught on the prairies as an iskwew, it's that you never go anywhere alone. This was always a warning we knew to be very real."

Jessica Johns, Bad Cree (p. 169)

This describes how reclamation is collective and not an individual journey. Mackenzie’s reconnection to her family allowed herself to transform into a crow healing her own identity. This reconnection was only ever possible through the willingness to share grief, dreams, and the truth, the moment she stopped being a Bad Cree.

Reclamation of Cultural Perspective

The journey of reclamation is only possible when Mackenzie reconnects with her sisters as she learns to reconnect with the land around her.

"We walk and talk and listen to the cars passing by sounding like rushing water. In the ambulance sirens from across town, I hear the sounds of the city calling me. In Kassidy's jokes, I hear Joli's teasing. As we move around town, I feel like the mountains are a little closer and the ground underneath us has the forgiving surface of sand. Our steps sure, our laughter carrying itself across the streets. I feel us come together, crows again."

(Johns 379)

The land itself holds cultural values through connecting Mackenzie back to her Cree identity. Even in the city, surrounded by concrete and sirens, her reconnection with her sisters allow her to perceive the world through a Cree lens. Reclamation isn’t a spiritual journey only, it’s a physical journey in which the individuals themselves must stand upon the land to truly grasp the surroundings.

For Mackenzie, her spiritual journey started with "red red red" (Johns 147) as Auntie Verna describes her as flickering and a warning to come, but it ends with "yellow yellow yellow" (Johns 370) as Mackenzie starts her reclaiming journey.

A depiction of a yellow crow, a healing crow (Anna)
Acceptance as the Final Act of Reclamation

As Mackenzie starts her reclamation journey through Auntie Verna's revolution of Mackenzie's healing through colours, the love heals as well and opens up Mackenzie's perspectives.

"I have so much love I'm sick with it. But there will always be bad living alongside it, etched under my skin. Living with bad doesn't make me bad, though, it's just there like everything else."

(Johns 381)

Mackenzie starts to recognize that reclamation starts with understanding the support around you rather than just the bad. The bad symbolizes the barriers worked against, whereas the love and support is the reclamation towards the barrier. For Indigenous people, the bad happens alongside the healing just as it did for Mackenzie, the true journey of reclamation. Today, Indigenous people continue to reclaim their identity through language, revitalization, community programs and literature. Bad Cree itself is proof, a Cree horror novel winning national awards and reaching mainstream literature.

Bad Cree as Reclamation Itself

Bad Cree started off as a short story, building to a novel showcasing how Indigenous author Jessica Johns took the recognition of the 2020 Writers’ Trust Journey Prize and transformed it into a mainstream published novel. This shows how Indigenous stories are no longer just confined to Indigenous spaces but working to reclaim space in Indigenous literature. As for the Indigenous communities, Bad Cree represents the modernization of reclamation, and so as in the ways of Mackenzie’s healing so should individuals strive towards their own reclamation by asking themselves what they have abandoned, lost, and what it would take to come together as crows.

Reclamation
The act of taking back what is rightfully yours whether that may be identity, language, land, story, or yourself.
Key Terms
Iskwew
The Cree word for woman. Used throughout the novel to signify the Cree language
Kokum
Cree word for grandmother. Kokum's presence in Mackenzie's dreams represents intergenerational knowledge and storytelling.
Wheetigo
A figure from Cree tradition representing the danger of disconnection and consuming greed.
Urbanization
The relocation of Indigenous peoples to cities, causing strain on cultures, languages, and traditional communities.
